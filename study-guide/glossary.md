# SecAI+ Glossary

> Unofficial study material aligned to CompTIA SecAI+ CY0-001 V1 objectives. See [../exam-objectives.md](../exam-objectives.md).

Terms are drawn from the official CY0-001 objectives across all four domains. Definitions are original and for study only.

## A

- **Accountability** — Responsible AI principle holding clearly identified people or teams answerable for an AI system's decisions, outcomes, and harms.
- **Accuracy** — Auditing dimension measuring how often a model's outputs are correct relative to ground truth or expected results.
- **Adversarial network** — An AI technique (often a generative model) used to deceive, evade, or attack another AI system, including crafting inputs that cause misclassification.
- **AI agents** — Autonomous or semi-autonomous AI systems that plan, reason, and invoke tools or APIs to accomplish tasks with limited human input.
- **AI architect** — Role responsible for designing the overall structure, components, and integration of an AI system.
- **AI auditor** — Role that independently evaluates AI systems for compliance, quality, fairness, and adherence to policy.
- **AI Center of Excellence** — Centralized organizational structure that sets AI strategy, standards, best practices, and governance across the enterprise.
- **AI cost monitoring** — Tracking AI-related spend across prompts, storage, responses, and processing to control usage and detect abuse.
- **AI governance engineer** — Role that implements and operationalizes governance controls, policies, and guardrails for AI systems.
- **AI policies and procedures** — Documented rules governing how AI is acquired, built, deployed, and used within an organization.
- **AI risk analyst** — Role that identifies, assesses, and reports on risks associated with AI systems.
- **AI security architect** — Role focused on designing and validating security controls that protect AI systems and data.
- **AI supply chain attack** — Attack targeting third-party models, datasets, libraries, or dependencies used to build or run an AI system.
- **Anomaly detection** — Identifying outliers or unusual patterns in data or behavior, frequently powered by machine learning.
- **Autonomous systems** — AI systems that act with minimal human intervention; a risk category due to reduced oversight of their decisions.
- **Awareness training** — Responsible AI practice of educating staff on AI risks, limitations, and acceptable use.

## B

- **Backdoor attack** — A hidden trigger implanted in a model during training that causes attacker-chosen behavior when a specific input is presented.
- **Bias (introduction of bias)** — Systematic skew in model behavior, introduced through data or design, that produces unfair or distorted outputs.
- **Bias and fairness** — Auditing dimension assessing whether a model treats individuals and groups equitably.
- **Business use case** — Lifecycle starting point that defines the AI system's purpose and its alignment with corporate objectives.

## C

- **Change management** — Controlled process for approving and deploying system changes, which AI can assist via automated approvals and deployment/rollback.
- **Chatbot** — Conversational AI application that responds to user prompts in natural language.
- **Circumventing AI guardrails** — Attack technique that bypasses a model's safety controls or restrictions.
- **CI/CD (continuous integration and continuous deployment)** — Automated pipeline for building, testing, and releasing software, including code scanning, SCA, and model testing for AI.
- **Code quality and linting** — Use case where AI reviews source code for defects, style issues, and maintainability problems.
- **Compensating control** — An alternative safeguard applied when a primary control is impractical, used to reduce residual risk.
- **Consistency** — Responsible AI principle that a model should produce reliable, repeatable results under similar conditions.
- **CVE AI Working Group** — Group addressing how the Common Vulnerabilities and Exposures program applies to AI/ML system vulnerabilities.

## D

- **Data anonymization** — Irreversibly removing or transforming identifying information so individuals cannot be re-identified.
- **Data augmentation** — Expanding a training set by generating modified copies of existing samples (e.g., rotations, paraphrases) to improve robustness.
- **Data balancing** — Adjusting class distribution in a dataset so over- or under-represented classes do not skew the model.
- **Data classification labels** — Tags applied to data indicating its sensitivity level and required handling.
- **Data cleansing** — Detecting and correcting errors, duplicates, and inconsistencies in data before use.
- **Data collection** — Lifecycle stage of gathering data, emphasizing trustworthiness and authenticity of sources.
- **Data engineer** — Role that builds and maintains the pipelines and infrastructure that move and prepare data.
- **Data integrity** — Property that data remains accurate, complete, and unaltered throughout its life.
- **Data lineage** — The documented history of data's origin and every transformation it undergoes as it moves through systems.
- **Data masking** — Replacing sensitive data values with obscured or fictitious equivalents while preserving usability.
- **Data minimization** — Collecting and retaining only the data strictly necessary for a defined purpose.
- **Data poisoning** — Corrupting training data to degrade model performance or implant malicious behavior.
- **Data provenance** — Documented record of data's source, ownership, and authenticity establishing where it came from and whether it can be trusted.
- **Data redaction** — Removing or blacking out sensitive content from data or documents.
- **Data scientist** — Role that designs, trains, and evaluates models and extracts insight from data.
- **Data sovereignty** — Principle that data is subject to the laws and regulations of the jurisdiction in which it is collected or stored.
- **Data verification** — Confirming that data is accurate, valid, and fit for its intended use.
- **Deep learning** — Machine learning using multi-layered neural networks to learn complex representations from data.
- **Deepfake** — AI-generated synthetic media (video, audio, image) that convincingly impersonates real people for impersonation, misinformation, or disinformation.
- **Differential privacy** — A privacy technique that adds mathematically calibrated noise so individual records cannot be inferred from aggregate outputs.
- **Disinformation** — Deliberately false or misleading information spread to deceive.
- **Distributed denial of service (DDoS)** — An attack that overwhelms a service with traffic from many sources to make it unavailable.

## E

- **Embeddings** — Numeric vector representations of text or other data that capture semantic meaning for search and retrieval.
- **Encryption at rest** — Protecting stored data by encrypting it on disk or in storage.
- **Encryption in transit** — Protecting data with encryption while it moves across networks.
- **Encryption in use** — Protecting data while it is being processed in memory (e.g., confidential computing).
- **Endpoint access controls** — Gateway controls that restrict which clients and identities can reach an AI endpoint.
- **Epoch** — One complete pass of the training algorithm over the entire training dataset.
- **EU AI Act** — European Union regulation that classifies and governs AI systems by risk tier, imposing obligations on higher-risk uses.
- **Excessive agency** — LLM risk where a system is granted too much functionality, permission, or autonomy, enabling harmful unintended actions.
- **Explainability** — The degree to which a model's decisions and behavior can be understood and interpreted by humans.

## F

- **Fairness** — Responsible AI principle that an AI system treats individuals and groups without unjust discrimination.
- **Federated learning** — Training a shared model across many decentralized devices or sites without centralizing the raw data.
- **Feedback and iteration** — Lifecycle stage where production feedback drives ongoing model refinement.
- **Fine-tuning** — Adapting a pretrained model to a specific task or domain by further training on targeted data.
- **Fraud detection** — Use case in which AI identifies fraudulent transactions or behavior patterns.

## G

- **Generative adversarial network (GAN)** — An architecture pairing a generator and a discriminator that compete, producing increasingly realistic synthetic data.
- **Generative AI** — AI that creates new content such as text, images, audio, or code from learned patterns.
- **Guardrail testing and validation** — Systematically probing a model's guardrails to confirm they block unsafe inputs and outputs as intended.

## H

- **Hallucination** — A model output that is fluent and plausible but factually incorrect or fabricated.
- **Honeypot** — A decoy system or asset deployed to attract and study attackers.
- **Human-in-the-loop** — Design pattern requiring a human to review or approve AI actions before they take effect.
- **Human oversight** — Ongoing human supervision and ability to intervene in or override an AI system.
- **Human validation** — Human review and confirmation of AI outputs before they are trusted or acted upon.

## I

- **Inclusiveness** — Responsible AI principle that systems should be accessible and equitable for diverse users and abilities.
- **Incident response ticket management** — Using AI to create, triage, enrich, and route security incident tickets.
- **Input manipulation** — Crafting or altering inputs to change a model's behavior or produce attacker-desired output.
- **Input quotas** — Gateway limits on the size and quantity of data a client may submit to an AI system.
- **Insecure output handling** — Failing to validate or sanitize model output before it is used downstream, enabling injection or other attacks.
- **Insecure plug-in design** — Plug-ins built with insufficient input validation or access control, expanding the model's attack surface.
- **Intellectual property (IP) risk** — Risk that AI exposes, infringes, or leaks proprietary or copyrighted material.

## J

- **Jailbreaking** — Crafting prompts that bypass a model's safety policies to elicit restricted or prohibited responses.

## L

- **Large language model (LLM)** — A transformer-based model trained on vast text corpora to understand and generate natural language.
- **Least privilege** — Granting an identity only the minimum access required to perform its function.
- **Log monitoring** — Continuously reviewing system and prompt logs to detect anomalies, abuse, or attacks.
- **Log protection** — Safeguarding logs against tampering, deletion, or unauthorized access.
- **Log sanitization** — Removing or masking sensitive data (e.g., PII, secrets) from logs before storage or sharing.

## M

- **Machine learning (ML)** — Algorithms that learn patterns from data to make predictions or decisions without explicit programming.
- **Machine learning engineer** — Role that builds, trains, and deploys ML models into production systems.
- **Membership inference** — Attack that determines whether a specific record was part of a model's training data.
- **MIT AI Risk Repository** — A curated catalog of documented AI risks used to support threat modeling and governance.
- **MITRE ATLAS** — Adversarial Threat Landscape for Artificial-Intelligence Systems, a knowledge base of real-world adversary tactics and techniques against AI.
- **Modality limits** — Controls restricting which input or output types (text, image, audio, etc.) a model will accept or produce.
- **Model access** — Access controls governing who and what may query or invoke a model.
- **Model Context Protocol (MCP) server** — A server implementing the open Model Context Protocol to expose tools, data, and context to AI applications in a standardized way.
- **Model denial of service (DoS)** — Attack that exhausts a model's compute, memory, or token budget to degrade or deny service.
- **Model development/selection** — Lifecycle stage of building a new model or choosing an existing one for the use case.
- **Model evaluation** — Assessing a model's performance, safety, and suitability against defined criteria.
- **Model guardrails** — Controls that constrain model behavior, such as prompt templates, content filters, and policy enforcement.
- **Model inversion** — Attack that reconstructs sensitive training data or attributes by probing a model's outputs.
- **Model poisoning** — Corrupting a model itself (weights or training process) to embed malicious or degraded behavior.
- **Model skewing** — Manipulating feedback or input data over time to gradually bias a model's outputs.
- **Model theft** — Stealing or extracting a model, its weights, or its behavior, often by systematic querying.
- **Model validation** — Verifying that a model meets accuracy, robustness, and requirement targets before deployment.
- **MLOps** — Practices and tooling for deploying, monitoring, and maintaining ML models in production.
- **MLOps engineer** — Role that builds and operates the pipelines, automation, and monitoring for production ML systems.
- **Misinformation** — False or inaccurate information spread regardless of intent to deceive.
- **Multi-shot prompting** — Prompting technique that supplies several examples to guide the model's response.

## N

- **Natural language processing (NLP)** — Field enabling computers to understand, interpret, and generate human language.
- **NIST AI RMF (AIRMF)** — NIST's AI Risk Management Framework organized around four functions: Govern, Map, Measure, and Manage.

## O

- **OECD AI Principles** — International principles from the OECD promoting trustworthy, human-centric, and accountable AI.
- **One-shot prompting** — Prompting technique that provides a single example to guide the model.
- **Output integrity attack** — Attack that manipulates or tampers with a model's outputs to mislead users or downstream systems.
- **Overreliance** — Risk of trusting AI outputs without adequate verification, leading to acceptance of incorrect or harmful results.
- **OWASP Top 10 (LLM / ML)** — OWASP-curated lists of the most critical security risks for LLM applications and for machine learning systems.

## P

- **Pattern recognition** — Identifying recurring structures or signatures in data, a common AI security use case.
- **Platform engineer** — Role that builds and maintains the underlying infrastructure and tooling supporting AI workloads.
- **Poisoning** — Family of attacks that corrupt training data (data poisoning) or the model (model poisoning).
- **Privacy and security** — Responsible AI principle that systems protect personal data and resist compromise.
- **Prompt engineering** — Crafting and structuring prompts (system, user, examples, roles) to elicit desired model behavior.
- **Prompt firewall** — Gateway control that inspects and filters prompts to block injection, jailbreaks, and policy violations.
- **Prompt injection** — Attack that embeds malicious instructions in a prompt or content to hijack a model's behavior.
- **Prompt monitoring** — Observing prompt queries and responses to detect abuse, leakage, or attack patterns.
- **Prompt templates** — Predefined, structured prompt formats that constrain and standardize model interactions.
- **Pruning** — Compressing a model by removing redundant or low-importance parameters to reduce size and cost.

## Q

- **Quantization** — Reducing the numeric precision of a model's weights (e.g., from 32-bit to 8-bit) to shrink it and speed up inference.

## R

- **Rate limiting** — Restricting the number of requests an identity may make in a period to prevent abuse and denial of service.
- **Rate monitoring** — Tracking request rates to detect spikes, abuse, or anomalous usage.
- **Reinforcement learning** — Training paradigm where an agent learns by maximizing reward signals from its actions.
- **Reliability and safety** — Responsible AI principle that systems perform dependably and avoid causing harm.
- **Reputational loss** — Risk that AI failures, bias, or misuse damage an organization's standing.
- **Response confidence level** — A score indicating how certain a model is about a given output, useful for triaging or flagging answers.
- **Responsible AI** — A set of principles (fairness, transparency, privacy, accountability, and more) guiding ethical AI design and use.
- **Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG)** — Technique that grounds an LLM's responses in external data retrieved from a vector store at query time.

## S

- **Semi-structured data** — Data with partial organization, such as JSON or XML, that lacks a rigid tabular schema.
- **Sensitive information disclosure** — Risk that a model reveals confidential, personal, or proprietary data in its outputs.
- **Shadow AI** — Unsanctioned use of AI tools or models outside organizational oversight; a form of shadow IT.
- **Shadow IT** — Technology used within an organization without approval or knowledge of IT/security.
- **Signature matching** — Detecting threats by comparing artifacts against known malicious patterns or signatures.
- **Small language model (SLM)** — A compact, resource-efficient language model suited to narrow tasks or edge deployment.
- **Social engineering** — Manipulating people into revealing information or taking actions, which AI can scale and personalize.
- **Software composition analysis (SCA)** — Scanning code dependencies to identify vulnerable or non-compliant open-source components.
- **Statistical learning** — Machine learning grounded in statistical models and inference to find structure in data.
- **Structured data** — Data organized in a fixed schema, such as relational database tables.
- **Supervised learning** — Training on labeled examples so a model learns to map inputs to known outputs.
- **System prompts** — Instructions that set a model's behavior, constraints, and context before user input.
- **System roles** — Defined roles in a conversation (e.g., system, user, assistant) that shape how the model interprets and responds.

## T

- **Templates** — Reusable prompt structures that standardize inputs to a model.
- **Threat modeling** — Structured process of identifying assets, threats, and mitigations for a system, including AI-specific resources.
- **Token limits** — Caps on the number of tokens allowed per request or response to control cost and abuse.
- **Transfer learning** — Reusing a model pretrained on one task as the starting point for a related task.
- **Transfer learning attack** — Exploiting vulnerabilities, biases, or backdoors inherited from a pretrained or transferred model.
- **Transformers** — Neural network architecture based on self-attention that underpins modern LLMs.
- **Transparency** — Responsible AI principle that an AI system's operation and decisions are open and understandable.
- **Trojan attack** — Embedding hidden malicious functionality in a model or component that activates under certain conditions.
- **Trustworthiness** — Property of data and models being authentic, reliable, and worthy of confidence.

## U

- **Unit testing** — Verifying individual code or model components in isolation, often automated within CI/CD.
- **Unstructured data** — Data without a predefined schema, such as free text, images, or audio.
- **Unsupervised learning** — Training on unlabeled data to discover structure such as clusters or patterns.

## V

- **Validation** — Lifecycle stage confirming a deployed model behaves correctly and meets requirements in its real context.
- **Vector storage** — A database optimized for storing and similarity-searching embeddings, central to RAG.
- **Vulnerability analysis** — Identifying and assessing weaknesses in code, systems, or models.

## W

- **Watermarking** — Embedding detectable markers in AI-generated content or models to establish provenance, ownership, or authenticity.

## Z

- **Zero-shot prompting** — Prompting a model to perform a task with no examples, relying solely on its pretrained knowledge.
